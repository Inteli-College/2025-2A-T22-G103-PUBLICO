\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates the impact of implementing digital signatures on latency and non-repudiation guarantees in high-frequency banking communications. The objective is to establish a methodological baseline for measuring computational costs, latency variation, and legal security gains.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} digital signatures, latency, non-repudiation, banking systems, high-frequency.

\section{Theoretical Foundation}
% WRITING ORDER: 1st - START HERE to build theoretical foundation
% Research and understand key concepts before designing experiments
Digital signatures are the cryptographic mechanism that binds a message to a signer identity while preserving integrity and verifiability. In banking communications, this property is central because transactions and acknowledgements must remain auditable under dispute, often under strict regulatory and operational controls. Classical schemes such as RSA \\cite{rsa1978} and elliptic-curve signatures such as ECDSA \\cite{ecdsa2001} are still widely used, while EdDSA-style constructions provide high-speed implementations and simpler deterministic signing behavior in practice \\cite{bernstein2012}. Current standards also emphasize algorithm agility, updated parameter requirements, and migration readiness, as reflected in FIPS 186-5 \\cite{fips1865} and the 2024 publication of post-quantum signature standards \\cite{fips204,fips205}.
